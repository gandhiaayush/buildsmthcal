# Design: Chatter — Shared-Audio AI Participant for Live Phone Calls

Generated by /office-hours on 2026-04-23

## Problem Statement

Two people on a regular carrier phone call (PSTN) — driving, commuting, or in transit — need a
fact mid-conversation. Siri is blocked during calls. Even if it weren't, only one person hears
the response. Google Assistant has the same limitation. The workaround is to pause the call,
search on one phone, and relay the information verbally — killing the flow and leaving one party
in the dark while the other searches.

Chatter joins the call as a third audible participant. Both parties hear it. Either can invoke
it. It has the full rolling transcript of the call as context.

Origin: Dhruva and Shivansh were driving, on a working call, needed to look something up.
Siri was blocked. Neither could pull out their phone safely. That's the product.

---

## Demand Evidence

- **Personal:** Two co-founders experienced this pain directly and repeatedly on working calls.
- **Validation status:** Soft only. People shown the concept think it's useful. No one outside
  the founding team has described this pain unprompted, offered to pay, or asked when it ships.
- **Critical next step:** Talk to 10 people who make frequent working calls (founders, researchers,
  professionals). Ask them to describe — unprompted — a time they needed information on a call
  and couldn't get it. If ≥7 have a specific, unprompted story, demand is real.
- **The Deutsche Telekom signal:** DT launched "Magenta AI Call Assistant" at MWC 2026 with
  ElevenLabs — network-embedded, both-parties-hear-it, wake word. Carrier-locked to T-Mobile
  Germany. Validates the concept is buildable and that the AI stack (ElevenLabs) works. Does
  not validate US consumer demand.

---

## Status Quo

Users on a working call needing a fact:
1. Interrupt the flow, ask the other party to hold
2. Search on one phone silently while the other party waits
3. Relay the information verbally (lossy, slow)
4. Or skip it entirely and lose the context

For drivers: option 2 means eyes off the road. For commuters on transit: noisy environment
makes it hard to hear search results being read back by a personal assistant only they can hear.

No dominant workaround exists — users do a combination of all of the above. No entrenched
habit to displace.

---

## Target User and Narrowest Wedge

**First user:** Startup founders and researchers who already use Claude actively, make
regular working calls (co-founder syncs, advisor calls, investor calls), and are sometimes
in transit during those calls. Not "regular B2C people" — people whose calls are
information-dense and where a wrong fact or a missed reference has real stakes.

**Narrowest wedge (Approach A):** A dial-in phone number. Party A and Party B both call
the number. They're now in a Twilio conference with Chatter as the third leg. Say "Hey
Chatter, [question]" and both hear the answer. No app, no install, no setup beyond sharing
a phone number. Party B needs nothing except a phone.

**The 48-hour test:** Can Dhruva and Shivansh use this on a real working call and feel
the magic? If yes, the core loop is validated. If no, the idea needs revision — not
more engineering.

## Constraints

- Legal: ECPA all-party consent required in some US states (CA strictest). Consent IVR flow:
  Chatter announces itself at call start ("This call includes an AI assistant. Press 1 to
  consent, or stay silent to disconnect."). State machine: both press 1 → active; one
  declines/times out after 10s → Chatter disconnects its leg; either party presses 2
  mid-call → Chatter mutes and announces departure. Legal review of all-party consent
  across all 50 states required before consumer launch. Budget: retain telecom attorney,
  ~$2-5k, before Approach B submission.
- Privacy: No training on call content. No third-party data sharing beyond infrastructure
  providers. Approach A uses Deepgram streaming STT (not Otter — Otter is batch-oriented
  and has known class-action liability). Otter eliminated from Approach A stack.
- Latency: Hard ceiling 2s end-to-end (STT → LLM → TTS → audio out). Must work on LTE.
  ElevenLabs Flash v2 outputs mulaw 8kHz audio — confirm Twilio media stream compatibility
  before build starts (known integration risk, add 2-day spike).
- Wake word: Config string for Approach A (not hardcoded to "Hey Chatter" — name is still
  TBD). Detection method: string match on Deepgram streaming transcript. Debounce: 500ms
  after phrase completes before triggering. False-positive handling: require exact
  phrase match, not partial. Formal phonetic uniqueness research before Approach B launch.
- Brand: "Claude" is a placeholder. "Chatter" is the working name. Final name and wake
  word are coupled — both TBD. Approach A uses a config string so name change requires
  no code change.
- Error handling minimum: STT dropout or API failure → Chatter says "Sorry, I couldn't
  get that — try again." Twilio leg drop → reconnect attempt once, then silent exit.
  Partial TTS → stop playback, do not stream silence.

## Premises

1. Deutsche Telekom proved the concept is buildable and validated the ElevenLabs stack.
   A carrier-agnostic, research-focused, US consumer app is an open lane.
2. "Students" is the wrong first niche. Founders/researchers on working calls who already
   use Claude actively are the real wedge.
3. PSTN conference bridge (carrier call, any phone) is the right MVP surface — matches
   the origin pain, covers the full installed base, works while driving.
   *(Subagent challenged this; founders defended with reasoning — driving/hands-free is
   the core differentiation. Zoom misses the origin use case.)*
4. B2C on-ramp → B2B vertical (life sciences, legal, finance) is the right architecture.
   Consumer proves the concept; B2B vertical is where defensible ARPU lives.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Minimal Dial-In Bridge (Recommended for immediate build)
- **Summary:** Twilio conference number. Both parties call in. Chatter is third leg.
  Wake word via transcript keyword spotter. One tool: web search.
- **Effort:** S (1-2 weeks, 1 engineer)
- **Risk:** Low
- **Pros:** Fastest to "feel the magic." No UI. Validates core loop at near-zero cost.
  Both founders can use it on a real call this week.
- **Cons:** Both parties must dial a number (friction vs. joining an existing call).
  Not the final consumer UX.
- **Stack:** Twilio Programmable Voice + Deepgram streaming STT + Claude Sonnet +
  ElevenLabs Flash v2 TTS (mulaw 8kHz output). Single Node.js service. Skip LiveKit
  (Twilio conference handles 3-party mixing natively). Skip Otter.
- **Service flow:** Twilio webhook → POST /voice → open Deepgram streaming STT on
  conference audio → rolling transcript (sliding 10-min window, older content
  summarized via Claude before dropping) → on wake-word match in transcript →
  LLM call with transcript context + web search tool → stream TTS response back
  via Twilio media stream → inject into conference audio.
- **Integration risk:** ElevenLabs Flash v2 → Twilio mulaw 8kHz format compatibility.
  Spike this first (1-2 days) before building anything else.
- **Completeness:** 4/10 — proves core, defers distribution UX

### Approach B: Companion App with Conference Merge (The real product)
- **Summary:** iOS/Android app for Party A. Already on a carrier call. App dials Chatter
  in via 3-way merge (native iOS/Android conference). Party B hears Chatter without
  any action.
- **Effort:** M (4-6 weeks)
- **Risk:** Med — iOS 3-way audio quality varies by carrier (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile
  must all be tested; Wi-Fi calling fallback if carrier audio mixing degrades quality)
- **Pros:** Closest to origin story UX. Party B needs nothing. Works on existing call.
  Real consumer product shape.
- **Cons:** App store submission adds time. iOS conference audio limitations. Must
  run carrier audio quality spike before committing to Approach B architecture.
- **Completeness:** 7/10 — real consumer UX, defers B2B and domain models

### Approach C: Browser-Based Shared Session (considered, not recommended)
- **Summary:** Party A opens web link, shares with Party B. Both join via browser WebRTC.
  Chatter in shared audio.
- **Effort:** S (1 week)
- **Risk:** Low-Med
- **Pros:** Zero install. Fast demo.
- **Cons:** Not a carrier call. Misses driving/hands-free use case entirely.
- **Completeness:** 3/10 — wrong surface for origin pain

## Recommended Approach

**A → B in sequence.**

Build Approach A in 48 hours. Use it on a real working call between Dhruva and Shivansh.
If both feel the magic — the rolling transcript context, the shared audio, the sub-2s
response — the core hypothesis is validated. Then build Approach B: the companion app that
merges Chatter into an existing carrier call without requiring Party B to do anything.

Approach A is the science experiment. Approach B is the product.

## Open Questions

1. **Wake word:** "Hey Chatter" is the candidate. Needs phonetic uniqueness research —
   is it parseable in a car at highway speed with road noise? Alternative candidates?
2. **Approach A → B transition (highest technical risk):** Approach A requires both parties
   to dial in. Approach B requires Chatter to merge into an *existing* call. iOS 3-way
   conference audio quality varies by carrier (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile). Required spike
   before Approach B build: test 3-way audio on all three carriers with a simulated Twilio
   leg. If audio degrades unacceptably: fallback is Wi-Fi calling (routes over data, bypasses
   carrier PSTN mixing). If Wi-Fi calling also fails: Approach B architecture needs redesign
   (possibly VAPI media streaming instead of native iOS conference).
3. **Consent IVR:** For MVP, a spoken disclaimer at call start ("This call includes an
   AI assistant. Both parties press 1 to consent...") is sufficient. Legal review of
   all-party consent mechanism across all 50 states before consumer launch.
4. **Transcription pipeline:** Deepgram streaming STT for Approach A (no Otter). Long-term:
   own STT pipeline with zero data retention. Evaluate AssemblyAI vs. Deepgram at scale.
5. **Pricing:** $5-10/month B2C (comparable to Wispr Flow). B2B tier pricing TBD.

**Future state (post B2C traction — out of scope for Approach A and B):**
- First B2B vertical: life sciences is sharpest candidate (Google Rosalind integration,
  researcher + PI on a call). Legal and financial data are alternatives.
- Multi-party calls (3+)
- FaceTime / Zoom / Teams integration
- Domain model plugins

## Success Criteria

**Approach A validation (48-hour test):**
- Two founders complete ≥1 real working call using Chatter
- Response delivered and audible to both parties (no audio dropout)
- End-to-end latency ≤2s measured on LTE (Deepgram streaming + Claude Sonnet + ElevenLabs)
- Zero call drops attributable to Chatter's Twilio leg
- Wake word detected correctly ≥3 times without false positives during the call

**Approach B launch (target: within 3 months of Approach A validation):**
- 100 active users making ≥2 calls/month with Chatter
- 10% conversion to paid tier
- NPS ≥ 40 from active users
- At least one user who would "be genuinely upset if it disappeared tomorrow"

**Pricing floor (required before launch):** Calculate cost-per-call (Deepgram streaming +
Claude Sonnet + ElevenLabs TTS API costs) for a 30-minute call. Ensure $5-10/month tier
has positive margin at target usage. This analysis must happen before pricing is finalized.

## Architecture Update — 2026-04-24

### The cost problem

After building Approach A: 2 test calls = $0.65 (~$0.33/call). Twilio is ~80% of
per-call cost. This is unviable at scale. A second /office-hours session evaluated four paths.

### Key technical constraint (confirmed)

iOS and Android do not allow third-party apps to intercept active carrier call audio.
The OS telephony stack owns the audio session — other apps are locked out. This rules
out the "app listens to the call and injects audio" vision entirely.

For person 2 to hear Chatter, audio must enter the carrier's phone network via 3-way
bridging. The choice is: which provider, and who initiates the bridge.

### Cost comparison (5-minute call)

| Path | Total/5-min call | vs. Twilio | Build time |
|------|-----------------|------------|------------|
| Twilio (current) | ~$0.33 | baseline | done |
| SignalWire (PSTN swap) | ~$0.19 | -42% | 1–2 days |
| LiveKit VoIP managed | ~$0.12 | -63% | 5–9 weeks |
| LiveKit VoIP self-hosted | ~$0.075 | -77% | 5–9 weeks |

AI costs (Deepgram + Gemini) are ~$0.067/5-min call regardless of path.

### Revised Approach A': SignalWire migration (immediate, 1–2 days)

Same PSTN conference bridge, different carrier. SignalWire is Twilio API-compatible.

**Migration steps:**
1. Create SignalWire account at signalwire.com
2. Provision phone number in SignalWire dashboard
3. `npm install @signalwire/compatibility-api`
4. `src/conference.js`: swap `require('twilio')` → `require('@signalwire/compatibility-api')`
5. Update `.env`: replace Twilio credentials with SignalWire equivalents
6. Update webhook URLs in SignalWire dashboard
7. Test call flow

Pricing: inbound $0.0066/min, outbound $0.0080/min (vs. Twilio $0.0085/$0.013/min)

### Revised Approach B': LiveKit VoIP — FaceTime link model (30-day roadmap)

Replace PSTN entirely with WebRTC. Person 1 uses an iOS app. Person 2 gets a link
(like a FaceTime link) and joins in Safari/Chrome with no install. Chatter AI is a
participant in the LiveKit WebRTC room. Both parties hear it.

```
Person 1 (iOS app)       ──WebRTC──┐
                                   ├── LiveKit Room ── Chatter AI
Person 2 (Safari/Chrome) ──────────┘
```

**Stack:** LiveKit Cloud + LiveKit Swift SDK (iOS) + LiveKit JS SDK (browser join page)
+ LiveKit Agents (replaces raw Deepgram WS) + Deepgram STT + Gemini + Deepgram TTS

**Timeline:** 5–9 weeks including App Store review (1–3 week review).

**Key trade-off:** Person 2 taps a link instead of receiving a normal phone call.
Test whether target users (founders, researchers) find this acceptable.

### Updated recommendation and sequencing

1. **This week:** SignalWire swap (1–2 days). Cost drops to ~$0.19/call.
2. **Before any new build:** 10-person demand validation (from original assignment).
3. **Month 2–3:** LiveKit iOS app if ≥7/10 interviewees have a specific unprompted story.

The original assignment is still the gate. Do not spend 6 weeks on LiveKit until
demand is confirmed outside the founding team.
